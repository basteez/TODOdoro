# Product Brief: tododoro

## Executive Summary

tododoro is a local-first, Pomodoro-enhanced todo app built on a single philosophy: your time and attention are acts of devotion, not units of output.

It helps people stay focused on what they genuinely care about — not through reminders, nudges, or gamification, but through honest reflection. Rather than managing tasks, tododoro becomes a personal mirror: it shows not just what you completed, but how much of yourself you gave to each intention, and where your presence actually went.

The app inverts the conventional productivity loop. Instead of planning tasks and filling them with time, you start with focus — and then reflect on what it meant. Todos are not obligations; they are declarations of personal ownership. Completion is not a checkbox; it is a sovereign act.

---

## Core Vision

### Problem Statement

Most productivity tools treat focus as a scheduling problem. Give tasks due dates, priority levels, and reminders, and the right things will get done. But the real problem isn't scheduling — it's that people accumulate intentions they don't genuinely own, spend their time on what's urgent rather than what matters, and have no honest record of where their attention actually went.

The result isn't inefficiency. It's a quiet disconnection from what you actually care about.

### Problem Impact

- People carry todo lists full of tasks that were never truly theirs
- Time spent is invisible — there is no mirror showing where attention went
- Completion feels hollow because nothing acknowledges what was invested
- Abandoning something feels like failure, even when it was the right decision
- Productivity tools reward output volume, not personal meaning

### Why Existing Solutions Fall Short

Traditional todo apps (Todoist, Things, OmniFocus) treat tasks as obligations — something to check off, prioritize, and schedule. They are task managers, not attention mirrors.

Pomodoro apps (Forest, Be Focused, Tomato Timer) treat time as a resource to manage, not devotion to track. They count sessions but never connect time to meaning.

Combined tools (like TickTick's Pomodoro integration) bolt focus sessions onto task lists as a feature — never as a philosophy. None of them answer the question: *"What have I truly shown up for?"*

### Proposed Solution

tododoro links every Pomodoro session to what you were present with. Over time, it becomes a Devotion Record — a visual history of how much of yourself you gave to each intention.

The Constellation Canvas replaces lists and tags with a spatial map of your intentions. Position is priority — drag a card to the center, that's what matters now. A session starts from the canvas, not from a checklist.

When you're done, you don't just check a box. You declare it complete. When you let something go, you choose how: because it no longer matters, or because it was never truly yours. Both are honored.

### Key Differentiators

1. **Session-first model** — Focus comes first; todos are labels you apply to focus, not containers you fill with time. This inverts the conventional productivity loop entirely.

2. **Two kinds of letting go** — The Release Ritual distinguishes between "completed its purpose" and "was never truly mine." No other productivity tool makes this distinction. It reframes quitting as an act of clarity, not failure.

3. **The Devotion Record** — A visual timeline of every Pomodoro invested in each intention. Not a count — a story. A todo completed in one session looks different from one that took three months of scattered effort.

4. **Radical respect for sovereignty** — No accounts, no cloud, no streaks, no scores, no nudges. Local-first and private by design. The user is the sole authority on their own achievement.

5. **Position is priority** — The Constellation Canvas eliminates an entire category of meta-work. No tags, no urgency levels, no priority fields. Spatial arrangement IS the priority system.

## Target Users

### Primary Users

#### Persona 1: Marco — The Tool-Fatigued Maker

**Background:** Marco is a 30-year-old freelance developer, designer, or indie builder. He works alone on self-directed projects. He is technically comfortable, aesthetically discerning, and privately exhausted by productivity tools that promise focus but deliver overhead.

**Context:** Marco has tried Things, Todoist, Notion, and two Pomodoro apps. None felt right. Things made him feel managed. Notion became a second job. Pomodoro apps counted sessions but said nothing meaningful about them. He keeps coming back to a plain text file. He is cynical about new tools — but he reads Hacker News, and something about tododoro's philosophy makes him try it anyway.

**Motivations:**
- Do deep, meaningful work on things he actually cares about
- Know, honestly, where his attention went
- Not feel guilty about letting something go when it was the right call
- Have a tool that respects him as an adult, not a user to be nudged

**How he experiences the problem:**
Monday morning. 47 items in his todo app. Some are weeks old. He doesn't know which ones are truly his. He picks something urgent, not something important. By Friday, he's been busy but doesn't know what he showed up for. The app says 12 tasks completed. It doesn't say what mattered.

**What success looks like:**
He opens tododoro and sees 6 cards — all ones he genuinely owns. He starts a session from the one that calls to him. At the end of the week, the Devotion Record shows exactly what he gave himself to. He releases two things without guilt. The canvas feels like his.

**"Aha" moment:** The first time he looks at a todo he's been quietly working on for three weeks and sees 11 Pomodoros across 9 days. No app has ever shown him that before. He sends it to a friend with no caption.

---

#### Persona 2: Sofia — The Invisible Worker

**Background:** Sofia is a 27-year-old PhD student, writer, or independent researcher. Her work is slow, non-linear, and hard to measure by output. Hours disappear into thinking, reading, and rewriting — and produce nothing checkable.

**Context:** Every productivity system she's tried was built for people whose work produces visible daily artifacts. Streaks break. Habit trackers shame her. She stopped using them. She keeps a paper notebook — it doesn't judge her.

**Motivations:**
- Acknowledge that she showed up, even when progress is invisible
- Feel that her invested time was witnessed somewhere, even if only by herself
- Let go of dead-end threads without it feeling like failure

**"Aha" moment:** tododoro shows her 23 Pomodoros invested in "Chapter 4 — methodology" across six weeks. The chapter isn't done. But the devotion is real, and the app witnesses it without judgment.

---

> **Note on the primary user split:** Marco is the go-to-market primary — he lives in the communities where tododoro will be discovered (Hacker News, indie makers, design Twitter). Sofia validates the philosophical soul of the product. Both are real. Marco spreads it; Sofia proves it matters.

> **The unifying thread:** The job title doesn't define these users. What defines them is their relationship to slow, invisible, non-linear work — and a belief that their attention is worth witnessing.

### Secondary Users

tododoro v1 is intentionally a single-user, personal tool — no collaboration, no team views, no sharing. The secondary audience is people who discover it through design or developer communities and become advocates before they become power users.

### User Journey

**Discovery:** Word of mouth in intentional-maker spaces — Hacker News "Show HN", designer Twitter/Mastodon, personal blog posts from people who explain *why* they switched. Not advertising. Philosophy spreads it.

**Onboarding:** There is none. Empty canvas, one ambient hint, 3 seconds. You understand immediately — or you are not the right user. This is intentional.

**Core Usage:** Open the canvas. See the constellation. Drag the most important card toward the center. Start a session. Work. Reflect. Repeat.

**The "Aha" Moment:** The first time the Devotion Record shows something true that no other tool ever has. The weight of what was given to a hard, slow thing.

**The Transformed User:** After a year, the Shelf is a record of a life — sealed todos with their full histories, released ones with their honest reasons. The user didn't journal. tododoro witnessed for them.

## Success Metrics

tododoro is a local-first, no-account personal tool. Traditional SaaS metrics (DAU, MRR, retention cohorts) don't apply — and applying them would contradict the product's philosophy. Success here is measured differently: by user truth, builder growth, and philosophical integrity.

### User Success

**The core signal:** A user opens tododoro on Day 8. Not because of a notification, a streak, or a reminder — but because the canvas is theirs and they want to return to it.

**Indicators of real value creation:**

- A user's canvas has fewer than 10 cards and they added none in the last week — because they released what wasn't theirs. **(Quality over accumulation)**
- A user has Devotion Records spanning weeks on a single intention. They didn't abandon it. They showed up. **(Depth of presence)**
- A user sends the app to someone with a description of *why* — not "it's a Pomodoro app" but something more personal. **(Philosophy resonance)**
- A user opens the Shelf and reads through it. **(The mirror is working)**

**Anti-metrics (signals of failure):**
- Users complain there's no due date field ← the philosophy is not resonating
- Users want sub-tasks ← wrong user, or wrong messaging
- Sessions under 60 seconds spike ← the minimum threshold is being gamed

### Business Objectives

tododoro v1 is a personal project with open-source intent. "Business" here means: does the builder ship, learn, and stay true to the vision?

| Horizon | Objective |
|---|---|
| **3 months** | A working v1 is live, deployed, and usable by Tiziano himself daily |
| **6 months** | tododoro is open source and has been shared publicly at least once (HN, blog post, or similar) |
| **12 months** | At least 10 people who are not Tiziano use it regularly and one of them has written or said something about why |

The 12-month objective is deliberately small. tododoro is not trying to win the productivity market — it is trying to find its people.

### Key Performance Indicators

**For the user (qualitative, not tracked automatically):**

| Signal | What it means |
|---|---|
| Canvas card count stays ≤ 10 over time | The user is curating, not accumulating |
| Devotion Records with 5+ Pomodoros exist | Deep, sustained focus is happening |
| The Shelf has entries | The lifecycle is being used fully |
| App is used without a Pomodoro timer open elsewhere | The timer is trusted |

**For the builder (quantitative, measurable):**

| KPI | Target | Timeframe |
|---|---|---|
| v1 shipped and self-hosted | ✓ | Month 3 |

| Public release (GitHub / HN) | ✓ | Month 6 |

**The one metric that matters most:**
Does Tiziano still use tododoro 6 months after shipping it? A tool its own builder abandons has not solved the problem. A tool its builder returns to daily has.

---

### Philosophical Integrity Metrics

These are not KPIs. They are guardrails — conditions that, if violated, indicate the product has drifted from its core.

- [ ] No account system, login, or server-side user data in v1
- [ ] No due date, priority field, or sub-task concept in the domain model
- [ ] No streaks, scores, or gamification of any kind
- [ ] Settings remain: timer durations + theme toggle, nothing else
- [ ] Canvas card hard cap (100) is never removed or made configurable
- [ ] "Done" is always a user declaration, never a system validation
